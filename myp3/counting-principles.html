🎲 Dice Strategy — Counting Your Chances

MYP3 Enrichment · Counting Principles · For students who finish class work early

Game Context: In Monopoly, you roll two dice and move that many spaces. Some properties get landed on way more often than others. Why? Let's use counting to find out.

🔑 Counting Principles

  • Fundamental Counting Principle: If event A has $m$ outcomes and event B has $n$ outcomes, then A and B together have $m \times n$ outcomes.
  • Factorial: $n! = n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \times \ldots \times 2 \times 1$
  • Permutations (order matters): $\P(n,r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$
  • Combinations (order doesn't matter): $\C{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$

📘 Worked Example — Two Dice

You roll two standard six-sided dice. How many ways can you get a sum of 7?

Step 1 — Total possible outcomes:

$6 \times 6 = 36$ total outcomes

Step 2 — List all ways to make 7:

Die 1123456
Die 2654321

6 ways to roll a 7

Step 3 — Probability:

$$\P(\text{sum} = 7) = \frac{6}{36} = \frac{1}{6} \approx 0.167$$
Game insight: 7 is the most likely roll with two dice. Properties 7 spaces from high-traffic spots (like Jail or Go) get hit more often.

📘 Worked Example — Three Dice (Permutations)

In some board games you roll three dice. What is the probability of rolling 1, 2, 3 in that exact order?

Step 1 — Total outcomes:

$6 \times 6 \times 6 = 216$ total outcomes

Step 2 — Favourable outcomes:

Only one way: (1, 2, 3)

Step 3 — Probability:

$$\P(1,2,3 \text{ in order}) = \frac{1}{216} \approx 0.00463$$

But what if order doesn't matter? How many ways can 1, 2, 3 appear in any order?

Step 4 — Count permutations of three different numbers:

$$3! = 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 6 \text{ ways}$$

Step 5 — New probability:

$$\P(1,2,3 \text{ any order}) = \frac{6}{216} = \frac{1}{36} \approx 0.0278$$
Game insight: In games where you need specific numbers but order doesn't matter, your chances are much better — 6 times better in this case!

📘 Worked Example — Yahtzee Full House (Combinations)

In Yahtzee, a "full house" is three of one number and two of another. What's the probability when rolling five dice?

Step 1 — Total outcomes:

$6^5 = 7776$ total outcomes

Step 2 — Choose which number appears 3 times:

6 choices (could be 1s, 2s, 3s, 4s, 5s, or 6s)

Step 3 — Choose which number appears 2 times:

5 remaining choices

Step 4 — Choose which 3 dice show the triple:

$$\C{5}{3} = \frac{5!}{3!2!} = 10 \text{ ways}$$

Step 5 — Total full house combinations:

$6 \times 5 \times 10 = 300$ ways

Step 6 — Probability:

$$\P(\text{full house}) = \frac{300}{7776} \approx 0.0386 \text{ or } 3.86\%$$
Game insight: A full house is surprisingly rare — less than 4% chance! Experienced players often aim for straights or four-of-a-kind instead.

Question 4 — Challenging[5 marks]

In Monopoly, players start at Go and roll two dice. Some properties are exactly 7 spaces away from Go:

  • Reading Railroad (space 5) is not 7 from Go
  • Chance (space 7) is exactly 7 from Go
  • Vermont Avenue (space 8) is 8 from Go

A property 7 spaces from Jail (space 10) would also be hit often because players frequently go to Jail and then roll from there.

(a) Find which property is exactly 7 spaces from Jail (space 10). (1 mark)

(b) Explain why properties near Jail might be better investments than properties near Go, using probability language. (2 marks)

(c) If you could only buy one property group, would you choose the group 7 spaces from Jail or 7 spaces from Go? Justify your answer with probabilities. (2 marks)
